## How to read this document

Each module section follows the same shape:

1. **One-line positioning** — what this module is *for* in one breath, derived from source.
2. **Source documents** — every PDF (or section of a PDF) that drives the module.
3. **At a Glance** — 3–5 scannable bullets you'd see at the top of the page.
4. **What reps need to know** — the body content. Section headings emerge from what the source PDFs actually teach, not from the current site's structure.
5. **Named landmines** — explicit pitfalls in ROE-style ("The X Trap" / "The Y Blender") — only where source material supports a real, concrete pitfall.
6. **Suggested Knowledge Checks** — 5–10 questions with answers and one-line explanations, each tagged with the source-document line that grounds it.
7. **Suggested Scenarios** — 2–3 named scenarios with 2–3 choices each.
8. **Source citations summary** — list of PDFs the section draws from.

The current site's video carousels, brick-breaker discovery breaks, Gantt charts, and other UI components are out of scope for this content design — that's structural, not content. This document focuses on **what should be on the page**, not how it's rendered.

# Module 2: Sales Rules of Engagement

**One-line positioning.** The territory and account-protection rulebook. *"Who owns what, when does an account open back up, and how do we resolve disputes — all sourced from Salesforce."*

**Source documents.**
- `Sales Rules of Engagement (ROE) - Revised August 2025 (1).pdf` (1 page) — the entire ROE fits on one page. Read it as the single source of truth.

## At a Glance

- **Salesforce is the official system of record** and will arbitrate any account dispute — period. *(ROE, "Source of Truth")*
- **Three activity windows govern everything:** Customer accounts open at **121 days** of no logged activity; Prospect accounts open at **61 days**; inbound leads can be reassigned after **5 business days** with no logged contact.
- **Strategic Accounts are protected by assignment, not by activity** — the 61/121-day rules don't touch them.
- **E-Rate and USAC-funded entities** (schools, libraries, hospitals) are Strategic Accounts by default, regardless of explicit list inclusion — except ENA (Education Networks of America, Indiana), which stays with the Out-State Team.
- **Account transfers require manager + sales leadership approval** — start with a conversation with the current owner, then email up the chain.

## What reps need to know

### The four guiding principles

The ROE opens with four general guidelines that frame the entire document:

1. **Source of Truth.** *"Salesforce is the official system of record and will be used to arbitrate any account disputes."* If it's not in Salesforce, it didn't happen.
2. **Account & Contact Creation.** Before creating a new account, search Salesforce by **both address and name** to prevent duplicates. Never open a second account record for an existing entity. Never add a new contact with a pre-existing email address — coordinate with the current account owner.
3. **Account Transfers.** Start with a conversation with the current owner. Once you've both agreed, email your manager **and** sales leadership for approval.
4. **Evolving Rules.** The ROE is designed to cover ~80% of situations. Unique scenarios go to sales management case-by-case. The document evolves — check the SharePoint for the current version.

### General accounts (non-Strategic)

The 61/121-day rules apply here. Three buckets:

**Customers.** An account in *Customer* status is **open to another rep at 121 days** of no logged activity. Best practice: even if it looks open, talk to the current owner before engaging.

**Prospects.** An account in *Prospect* status is **open at 61 days** of no logged activity. Critical nuance from the ROE: *"You must keep your accounts updated with relevant information, such as construction projects or contract dates. However, notation alone does not protect an account; consistent contact (at minimum, every 60 days) is required."* You cannot park an account with a single Salesforce comment and call it protected.

**Inbound Leads.** A lead assigned by sales or marketing leadership can be **reassigned after 5 business days** if initial contact hasn't been made and logged. This **5-day rule does not apply** to leads you generated yourself through prospecting.

### Strategic Accounts

The Strategic Accounts team and the named-list rules:

- **Protection by assignment, not by activity.** Strategic Accounts are not subject to the 61/121-day rules. The list is dynamic, reviewed and amended by the Sales Managers and the VP of Sales, and housed in the ROE SharePoint repository.
- **E-Rate / USAC entities default to Strategic.** All entities — schools, libraries, hospitals — that currently utilize or are eligible for E-Rate or USAC funding are designated Strategic, regardless of explicit list inclusion. **One named exception:** ENA (Education Networks of America), based in Indiana, remains under the Out-State Team's purview.

### How disputes get resolved

The ROE doesn't spell out a multi-step escalation tree because the General Guidelines already handle it:

1. **Salesforce arbitrates** — whoever has the activity log wins the call.
2. **Rules cover ~80% of cases.** The remaining 20% goes to sales management.
3. **Account transfers always require manager + sales leadership.** Don't transfer informally rep-to-rep.

## Named landmines

- **The Duplicate Account.** A rep skips the Salesforce search and creates "ABC Manufacturing Inc" when an account already exists as "ABC Manufacturing." Two reps now think they own the same logo. The ROE says: *search by address and name before creating anything.*
- **The Notation-Is-Protection Trap.** A rep drops a note into Salesforce that says "construction project Q3" and assumes that note alone protects the account through the 61-day window. The ROE explicitly says it doesn't: *"notation alone does not protect an account; consistent contact (at minimum, every 60 days) is required."*
- **The Casual Transfer.** Two reps shake hands over coffee and "transfer" an account. Nothing happens in Salesforce. Three months later, the original rep gets paid on a deal the other rep closed. Always loop in manager + sales leadership and document the move.

## Suggested Knowledge Checks

1. **Q.** A Prospect account has had no logged activity for 60 days. Is it open for another rep to work? **A.** Not yet. The Prospect window is 61 days. *(ROE, General Accounts → Prospects)*
2. **Q.** A Customer account has had no activity logged for 90 days. Is it open? **A.** No. The Customer window is 121 days, not 61. *(ROE, General Accounts → Current Customers)*
3. **Q.** You generated a lead yourself through prospecting and haven't logged contact in 6 days. Can it be reassigned? **A.** No. The 5-day reassignment rule applies only to leads assigned by sales or marketing leadership, not self-generated leads. *(ROE, General Accounts → Inbound Leads)*
4. **Q.** Are all E-Rate–funded entities Strategic Accounts? **A.** Yes, by default — schools, libraries, hospitals utilizing or eligible for E-Rate or USAC funding are Strategic, regardless of explicit list inclusion. *(ROE, Strategic Accounts → E-Rate & USAC Funded Entities)*
5. **Q.** What is the one named exception to the E-Rate/USAC Strategic default? **A.** ENA (Education Networks of America), based in Indiana — they remain under the Out-State Team's purview. *(ROE, Strategic Accounts)*
6. **Q.** True or false: a Salesforce comment alone protects a Prospect account through the 61-day window. **A.** False. Consistent contact at minimum every 60 days is required; notation alone does not protect. *(ROE, General Accounts → Prospects)*
7. **Q.** What is required to transfer an account from one rep to another? **A.** A conversation with the current owner first, then email approval from manager **and** sales leadership. *(ROE, General Guidelines → Account Transfers)*

## Suggested Scenarios

### Scenario A — "The Cold Customer"

You spot an account in Salesforce: large local manufacturer, status *Customer*, last activity logged 95 days ago. The owning rep is on vacation. What do you do?

- **(a) Reach out to the prospect directly — it's been 95 days, no harm done.** *Wrong — the Customer window is 121 days, not 90. The account is still protected.*
- **(b) Wait until day 122 and then engage without telling anyone.** *Wrong — even if it's technically open at 122, ROE best practice is to talk to the current owner first.*
- **(c) Wait until the owning rep is back, have a direct conversation, document agreement, and confirm with manager.** *Right — respects both the activity window and the relationship-first principle in the ROE.*

### Scenario B — "Is This Strategic?"

A new lead comes in: a rural K-12 school district in Iowa, not on the published Strategic Accounts list. You're on the General team. Can you work it?

- **(a) Yes — they're not on the named list.** *Wrong — E-Rate eligibility makes them Strategic by default.*
- **(b) No, but only if they're currently using E-Rate funding.** *Partially wrong — the ROE says "utilize OR are eligible" for E-Rate/USAC. Eligibility alone is enough.*
- **(c) No — schools eligible for E-Rate are Strategic by default. Send the lead to the Strategic Accounts team.** *Right.*

# Module 3: Sales Process & Salesforce

**One-line positioning.** The end-to-end mechanics of moving a deal from Lead → Account/Contact/Opportunity → Solution → Close-Won → Change Order (or Disconnect), all inside Salesforce. *"How the system thinks, so you can stop fighting it."*

**Source documents.**
- `Converting Leads to an Opportunity Quick Reference Guide - 1123.pdf` — the conversion mechanic.
- `Converted Lead Info Button Quick Reference Guide - 1123.pdf` — how to see Lead history after conversion.
- `Contact Roles Quick Reference Guide - 1023.pdf` — Sales' Main Contact, Decision Maker, Influencer, Project Contact, etc.
- `Change Order Process - Sales Team - 0325.pdf` — what happens between Closed/Won and Billing.
- `Costing Routing Quick Reference Guide - 1123.pdf` — New vs. Existing services, OSP automated costing, Strategic Build, Reusable.
- `Create a Custom List View Quick Reference Guide - 0922.pdf` — daily-driver list views.
- `Parent Opportunity Process Training Guide - 0822.pdf` and `Parent Opportunity Financial Calculations Quick Reference Guide - 1023.pdf` — Zone Parent Opportunities for multi-site deals.
- `Salesforce 101 Manual - 0422.pdf` — the foundational SF reference.

## At a Glance

- **Lead → Opportunity is a one-way door.** Once you Convert, the Lead record disappears and information is mapped to the Contact and Opportunity. To recover Lead context post-conversion, use the **Converted Lead Info button** on the Opportunity.
- **GPC's Salesforce isn't vanilla** — it adds GPC Solutions, Costing Routing, Parent Opportunities (for multi-site Zones), Conga document templates, and a Salesforce/NISC iVUE integration that bridges sales to provisioning/billing.
- **Contact Roles drive downstream workflows.** Setting Sales' Main Contact, Decision Maker, Project Contact, Billing Contact, etc. is what lets PMO and Business Customer Care do their jobs after Close-Won.
- **Change Orders only work between Closed/Won and Booked-but-not-Billed.** After billing starts, the change becomes a Move/Add/Change or a Disconnect — different process, different team.
- **Strategic Build, Zone Parent, and Reusable** are three automation flags that change the routing path — knowing which one applies saves days in approval cycles.

## What reps need to know

### The deal lifecycle, in five stages

1. **Lead.** Created by CRC via a SharePoint Lead form, by Marketing campaigns, or manually in Salesforce. Source matters — Employee Business Lead Referrals and Partner Referrals have payment implications.
2. **Conversion.** Click *Convert Lead* on the Lead page. Three sections: Account (new or existing), Contact (new or existing), Opportunity (almost always new). Best practice: *always* create a new Opportunity, *never* check "Don't create an opportunity upon conversion." Update the Opportunity Name to something that references the specific chance to sell to this specific customer.
3. **Opportunity → GPC Solution(s).** Each Opportunity carries one or more Solutions. Costing Routing runs against each Solution based on Site Type + Services + Products selected.
4. **Closed/Won.** Required fields complete. Contact Roles set (Sales' Main Contact, Decision Maker minimum; Project Contact, Billing Contact, Property/On-Site Manager, IT/Technical, Phone System Contact as applicable).
5. **Change Order or Disconnect.** A change *between* Closed/Won and Billing follows the Change Order process. A change *after* Billing is a Move/Add/Change or Disconnect — different process.

### Contact Roles — the part that bites you later

The Contact Roles QRG defines nine roles. Per the QRG, **Project Contact, Billing Contact, and Property Manager / On-Site Manager are required on every Solution you Close-Won.** Reps who skip Contact Roles leave PMO, CRC, and Business Customer Care guessing — which means installs get delayed and bills go to the wrong inbox.

The nine roles, verbatim from the QRG:

- **Sales' Main Contact** — person the Account Executive reaches out to about the sale or contract.
- **Influencer** — encourages or recommends GPC to the Decision Maker.
- **Decision Maker** — signs the contract; may or may not be the owner.
- **Project Contact** — main point of contact for PMO; provide for every Solution you Close-Won.
- **Billing Contact** — PMO or Business Customer Care contact for billing; provide for every Solution you Close-Won.
- **Property Manager / On-Site Manager** — for site access; provide for every Solution you Close-Won.
- **General Contractor** — only when construction is needed on a Project.
- **IT / Technical / Data Contact** — coordinate installation with the customer's IT contractor.
- **Phone System Contact** — coordinate Voice or Phone System installations (not needed for UC products).
- **Agent (Channel)** — Channel Sales only.
- **Business User** — the person actually using the Services/Product.
- **Other** — use only when nothing else fits.

Best practice from the QRG: set **Status to Active** on the Contact record, and set **Preferred Contact Method** with the matching contact information populated.

### Costing Routing — New vs. Existing, and the three automation flags

The Costing Routing QRG distinguishes:

- **New Services** — choose when Services require a new install connection or the customer is new.
- **Existing Services** — choose when Services are currently provided and you're upgrading/downgrading, adding features, etc.

Three flags can change the routing path:

1. **Strategic Build.** Solution Sites are flagged Strategic Build only if GPS Coordinates fall within geographical boundaries pre-loaded into the system. When flagged, **Individual Drop Cost and Individual Equipment Cost are applied systematically** — bypassing a step that normally requires OSP review.
2. **Zone Parent Opportunity.** Any Solution Site associated with a Zone Parent Opportunity **may bypass automation in routing.** Reusable is **not allowed** if the Solution Site is associated with a Zone Parent Opportunity.
3. **Reusable.** Available when specific criteria are met; lets OSP and Network Engineering complete costing routing on the team's behalf via the Reusable matrix.

Two rules every rep should memorize:

- **If New and Existing Services are selected for the same Solution Site, routing follows the New Services path.**
- **If multiple Services are selected, routing follows the path of the Service needing the most in-depth review.**

### Change Orders — the narrow window between Close-Won and Billing

The Change Order QRG is explicit about the window:

> *"A Change Order is used when the customer needs to make a change to their contract after it has been Closed/Won but before Billing has started. If the customer is changing only a part of their original contract after billing has started that is a Move/Add/Change… If the customer is removing all services and products from their contract before billing has started that is a Cancellation… after Billing has started that is a Disconnect."*

The process, in seven steps from the QRG:

1. Confirm GPC Solution is in Closed/Won stage and **Booked but not Billed**. Change Orders cannot be processed once Billed.
2. Email your Sales Manager if the change is significant or has significant impact on MRC, Payback, or additional costs.
3. Navigate to the GPC Solution → click **Conga Files** → select **Change Order** from the template list → Preview.
4. Open and edit the form: Solution Site Affected, Description of Change, Specific Changes. For Change Orders adding a Product not currently on the GPC Solution, **provide the Product's correct link in Salesforce** — make sure the Product record correlates with the Solution Site's Market Group for accurate MCOS, NCOS, and other financial information.
5. Save → print or send to the customer for signature → add your signature and date.
6. Upload the signed form to the GPC Solution.
7. Email the signed form + GPC Solution link to: your Sales Manager (if step 2 applied), **PMO, Salesforce Administrator, CRC**.

Team responsibilities post-submit:

- **Salesforce Administration** — makes the Booking Adjustment.
- **PMO** — updates the project.
- **CRC** — handles any billing changes.
- **Special case:** if the Solution Site Project Stage is in *Pending Billing* or *7-10 Day Order*, Salesforce Admin must request approval from PMO/CRC before proceeding.

### The Converted Lead Info button — a small thing that saves real time

Once a Lead is converted, the Lead record is gone and the data is mapped to Contact and Opportunity. The **Converted Lead Info button**, on the Opportunity, exposes the original Lead context (comments, source, referral information). Reps who don't know about this button manually re-collect information they already had.

To view long comments truncated in the report: Edit drop-down → Export → Details Only → open in Excel.

### Zone Parent Opportunities

For multi-site deals, GPC uses a **Zone Parent Opportunity** that ties multiple child Solutions together. Two important nuances:

- Solution Sites under a Zone Parent **may bypass automation in routing.**
- **Reusable is not allowed** under a Zone Parent.

The Parent Opportunity Process and Financial Calculations QRGs are the detailed references for multi-site sales.

## Named landmines

- **The "Don't Create an Opportunity" Box.** During Lead conversion, there's a checkbox labeled *"Don't create an opportunity upon conversion."* The QRG says: *"Almost never click the box."* Reps who tick it lose the lead-to-deal connection in Salesforce and Marketing loses attribution.
- **The Wrong-Market-Group Product Link.** On a Change Order adding a new Product, the QRG warns: *"Be sure to choose the Product record that correlates with the Solution Site's Market Group for accurate MCOS, NCOS, and other financial information."* Reps who pick a Product from the wrong Market Group blow the Solution's financials and the Change Order gets bounced back.
- **The Forgotten Property Manager.** Project Contact, Billing Contact, and Property Manager are required on every Closed-Won Solution. Skipping the Property Manager doesn't fail the close — it stalls the install, because PMO can't reach the building.
- **The Bill-Has-Started Change.** A rep tries to file a Change Order for a Solution that's already Billed. The Change Order system rejects it; the change needs to go through Move/Add/Change instead. The QRG is explicit about the **Booked-but-not-Billed** boundary.

## Suggested Scenarios

### Scenario A — "The Change That Almost Wasn't"

You closed a 5-site deal three weeks ago. Today the customer calls — they want to swap one of the sites from a 1 Gbps DIA to a 500 Mbps DIA. You check Salesforce: the Solution Site is Closed/Won, Booked, and the Project Stage is *Pending Billing*. What do you do?

- **(a) File a Change Order immediately.** *Partially right — you're in the right window (Booked, not Billed), but Pending Billing triggers an extra approval. The Change Order QRG says: "If the Solution Site Project Stage is in either 'Pending Billing' OR '7-10 Day Order', Salesforce Admin will need to request approval from PMO/CRC before proceeding with the booking adjustment." Flag it to your manager first.*
- **(b) Tell the customer it's too late — they have to wait until billing starts and submit a Move/Add/Change.** *Wrong — Change Orders are still valid; you're just in a special case.*
- **(c) Submit a Disconnect and create a new Solution.** *Wrong — that's the heavy hammer; the customer wants to change, not cancel.*

### Scenario B — "The Phantom Lead"

# Module 4: Sales Operations & Approvals

**One-line positioning.** The deal-shape rulebook. *"What auto-approves, what needs Manager / CRO / CFO / CEO sign-off, and how the Payback Months / Discount / Net Investment / Churn thresholds actually work."*

**Source documents.**
- `Approval Requirements Matrix v5.5 - 0725.pdf` — the matrix itself. Multiple appearances across `OneDrive_1_4-10-2026 (2)` and `(3)` folders confirm v5.5 - 0725 is the current version.

## At a Glance

- **Payback Months auto-approve thresholds scale with contract term** — short-term deals (0–23 months) auto-approve only at ≤1 Payback Month; 120+ month terms auto-approve up to 44 Payback Months. Anything above the threshold needs Manager review.
- **Discount auto-approve thresholds also scale with term.** Products that aren't discountable-by-term cap at 20%. Discountable-by-term products go from 20% (0–23 mo) up to 31% (60+ mo).
- **Net Investment escalation ladder:** Auto-approve <$50k → Manager review ≥$50k → CRO review ≥$75k → CFO review ≥$100k → CEO review ≥$500k.
- **Churn caps at 10%.** Auto-approves at ≤10%; above triggers Manager review (and CRO review for higher bands).
- **Two flags bypass Payback Months entirely:** *Strategic Build* Solution Site Type, and the *Bulk Process* used by Wholesale (which also bypasses Product Discount approval).
- **Dark Fiber always requires both Manager and CRO review** — regardless of payback, discount, or investment numbers.

## What reps need to know

### The five things the matrix evaluates

Every deal that goes through the Approval Requirements Matrix is evaluated against five categories — plus two product-specific overrides:

1. **Payback Months by Term**
2. **Product Discount**
3. **Net Investment**
4. **Churn**
5. **Unfavorable Investment**

Plus:

- **Product** (review required by Product Manager for certain products)
- **Dark Fiber** (always Manager + CRO)
- **Zone Parent Opportunity** (Net Investment thresholds apply at the Parent level)

### Payback Months — the term-scaled ladder

A deal auto-approves on Payback Months only if Payback ≤ the auto-approve number for the contract term:

| Contract term (months) | Auto-approves if Payback Months ≤ |
| --- | --- |
| 0–23 | 1 |
| 24–35 | 12 |
| 36–47 | 18 |
| 48–59 | 24 |
| 60–71 | 30 |
| 72–83 | 31 |
| 84–95 | 33 |
| 96–107 | 38 |
| 108–119 | 40 |
| 120+ | 44 |

Above the threshold → **Manager approval review required.** Two bypasses:

- **Strategic Build Solution Site Type bypasses Payback Months entirely.**
- The Bulk Process used by Wholesale is not evaluated by the Payback Months requirement.

### Product Discount — discountable-by-term vs. not

The matrix distinguishes two product classes:

- **Discountable, not by Term** — auto-approve cap is **20%**, regardless of contract length. Above 20% → Manager review.
- **Discountable by Term:**

  | Contract term (months) | Auto-approves if Discount ≤ |
  | --- | --- |
  | 0–23 | 20% |
  | 24–35 | 22% |
  | 36–47 | 27% |
  | 48–59 | 29% |
  | 60+ | 31% |

Above the threshold → Manager review. **Wholesale Bulk Process is not evaluated against the Product Discount requirement.**

### Net Investment — the four-tier escalation

| Net Investment | Approval level |
| --- | --- |
| < $50,000 | Auto-approves |
| ≥ $50,000 | Manager review |
| ≥ $75,000 | CRO review |
| ≥ $100,000 | CFO review |
| ≥ $500,000 | CEO review |

This is the ladder reps memorize because customers ask about it. The numbers are absolute, not bands — at $76k, you need Manager **and** CRO. At $501k, you need all four.

### Churn — the 10% line

Auto-approves at ≤10%. Above 10% → Manager review. Higher bands also trigger CRO review (the matrix shows the same 10% number across all auto-approve / Manager review / CRO review columns, indicating churn above 10% is treated as material across the chain).

### Unfavorable Investment & Product

- **Unfavorable Investment** — Manager review required.
- **Product** — certain Products require Product Manager review.
- **Dark Fiber** — **Both Manager and CRO review required**, regardless of any other metric. This is the one product-line override that always applies.

### Zone Parent Opportunity

Zone Parent Opportunity evaluates **Net Investment value at the Parent level** — meaning a multi-site deal's escalation uses the aggregate Net Investment of all child Solutions, not each Solution individually.

### Two important universal rules

1. **Strategic Build bypasses Payback Months.** If the Solution Site is flagged Strategic Build, Payback Months is not evaluated — the build investment already passed a separate strategic gate.
2. **Wholesale Bulk Process bypasses both Payback Months and Product Discount.** Wholesale operates under a different commercial model; the Bulk Process is the carve-out.

## Named landmines

- **The Auto-Approve Eyeball Test.** A rep eyeballs a 36-month deal and sees "Payback Months = 16" and thinks "we're fine." The matrix says 36–47 month auto-approve is ≤18, so 16 is fine on Payback — but the rep forgot to check the Discount column (the deal had a 28% discount on a discountable-by-term product, which exceeds the 27% auto-approve for 36–47 months). One auto-approve in one column does not mean the whole deal auto-approves.

## Named landmines

- **The Plan Ladder Blur.** Reps quote "UC" without specifying a tier. Customer signs, expects Pro features, gets Essentials. Always anchor to the tier: *"UC Essentials includes…"* not just *"UC includes…"*.
- **The License Blender.** A customer asks for UC Pro for 30 users plus UC Express for 5 receptionist phones plus 5 Resource Lines for front-desk and lobby phones. The plan-comparison sheet says **plans can be mixed and matched per user** — but reps who don't blend correctly often over-license, paying for Pro features on phones that just need to ring.
- **The CCaaS Trap.** A customer says "I need a call center." A rep quotes UC Pro because UC Pro includes Advanced Hunt Groups (Call Center). That's the **light** call center feature. If the customer needs queues across voice / chat / email / SMS, supervisor barge-in, outbound dialer, etc., that's **Contact Center (CC Pro or CC Elite)**, not UC Pro. The Contact Center datasheet is explicit that CC is sold **with UC or standalone.**
- **The Microsoft Native Trap.** A customer says "we have Microsoft 365 + Teams + Teams Phone — we're set." Reps who fold lose the deal. The defensible answer: most Teams users **don't** activate Teams Phone (the cost-to-feature gap is real), and **UC for Microsoft Teams** keeps Teams as the collaboration hub while bringing in GPC UC's enterprise PBX. Customer keeps their Teams investment and gets the PBX feature set they actually need.
- **The 200 GB Brochure Trap.** The UC Brochure's footnote says 200 GB storage is **Enterprise only.** Reps who quote 200 GB on a Pro proposal create a clawback. 50 GB on Pro; 5 GB on Essentials; **200 GB only on Enterprise.**
- **The "99.999% Means Always" Mistake.** Five nines of uptime is **5.26 minutes of downtime per year** — not "zero downtime." Customers (especially IT directors) catch reps who say "we never go down." The honest line: *"99.999% uptime backed by the platform's SLA — that's less than six minutes a year, on average."*

# Module 12: Account Based Marketing

**One-line positioning.** The 90-day, multi-thread motion for breaking into Strategic accounts — *"3 active ABM accounts per rep on a 30-day staggered kickoff, 3 rolling On-Deck accounts, 18 sales touches + 3 marketing touches per account over 90 days, all tracked in the Master Spreadsheet."*

**Source documents.**
- `ABM SOP.pdf` (`~/Downloads/ABM SOP.pdf`, 3 pages) — the complete SOP. Authored by Bryan Anneler, version 1.0 (2026-01-22). Single source of truth for the entire motion.

## At a Glance

- **The cadence is fixed:** 3 sales touches per target every 2 weeks × 5 targets max = **up to 18 sales touches over 90 days**; **1 Marketing touchpoint every 30 days** (LinkedIn/Google ads or direct mail). The "Door Pull" optional move adds a Marketing touchpoint as a leave-behind insert.
- **The Rolling Three keeps the pipeline alive.** Each rep manages **3 concurrent ABM accounts** with **3 On-Deck accounts** waiting in the wings — the staggered 30-day kickoff means one account is always in high-intensity launch while others are mid-cycle or wrapping.
- **Multi-threading is mandatory.** Minimum 3 targets per account (5 ideal), and **they cannot all be Executive level** — the required mix is Manager (Operational/Technical) + Director/VP (Tactical/Strategic) + Executive (Financial/Final Decision Maker).
- **The Kickoff is a real meeting, not a checkbox.** Marketing + Sales Manager + Account Rep all attend; the rep arrives with the Sales Readiness Checklist completed (Account Intel, Status, Current Services, Location Scope, Prospecting History, finalized Target List).
- **The Master Tracking Spreadsheet is the source of truth** — View-only for general stakeholders; Edit access for Marketing and Sales Leadership only.

## What reps need to know

### What ABM is, and what it's for

From the SOP's overview (§1):

> *"The Account Based Marketing (ABM) process is a high-touch, 90-day coordinated effort between Sales and Marketing to penetrate high-value Strategic accounts. By synchronizing 18 personal sales touches with consistent marketing air cover, we ensure GPC remains top-of-mind for key decision-makers."*

ABM is reserved for **Strategic accounts** (see ROE, Module 2). It is not a general prospecting motion; it's the deliberate, slow-burn, multi-threaded campaign for accounts that warrant the investment.

### Rep workload — the Rolling Three model

The SOP is explicit about the workload (§2 and §3):

- Each Account Rep manages **3 concurrent ABM accounts** at any given time.
- These three accounts are on a **staggered 30-day kickoff cycle:** Account A kicks off in Month 1; Account B in Month 2; Account C in Month 3.
- **Pipeline Management — the "Rolling Three":** Sales must maintain **3 rolling On-Deck accounts** to backfill the active tracks. As one account completes its 90-day cycle, the next On-Deck account is onboarded immediately.

The example the SOP uses: *"As the February kickoff (Account A) completes its 90-day cycle in April, the first backfill account is onboarded for a May kickoff."*

The staggered design means **one account is always in the high-intensity initial phase** while others are in mid-cycle or wrapping — preventing simultaneous "all-in" sprints across multiple accounts.

### Target selection — the tiered hierarchy

From §4. Two rules:

1. **Target Volume:** Minimum of 3 targets per account, **5 is ideal.**
2. **Tiered Hierarchy:** Targets cannot all be Executive-level. The minimum-3 mix must include:
    - **Manager (Operational/Technical)** — the person feeling the pain day-to-day.
    - **Director / VP (Tactical/Strategic)** — the person who owns the budget area.
    - **Executive (Financial/Final Decision Maker)** — the person who signs.

The mandate is **multi-threaded engagement to avoid single-point-of-failure risk** — if your one Executive contact retires or gets reassigned mid-cycle, you don't lose the account.

### The Kickoff — what's required before the 90-day clock starts

The Kickoff meeting officially initiates the 90-day clock (§5). Three required attendees:

- **Marketing**
- **Sales Manager**
- **Account Representative**

The Sales Readiness Checklist (mandatory for the rep before Kickoff):

- **Account Intel** — comprehensive research and general background on the account.
- **Status** — Current customer vs. Prospect.
- **Current Services** — where are their current services (if applicable/known).
- **Location Scope** — density and geographic diversity of the account's locations.
- **Prospecting History** — audit of previous attempts (calls, emails, LinkedIn, physical drop-ins).
- **Target List** — finalized list of 3–5 specific targets and their roles.

If the rep shows up without these, Marketing can't build the campaign air cover and the Kickoff was wasted.

### The 90-day Execution Sequence

From §6. Two simultaneous cadences:

**Sales Cadence:**

- **Frequency:** 3 sales touches per target every 2 weeks.
- **Total volume:** 18 total sales touches over the 90-day period (3 touches × 3 targets × 2-week cycles fitting in 90 days).
- **Primary channels:** Phone calls and personalized emails.
- **Optional "Door Pull":** Physical drop-by using the Enterprise Brochure.

**Marketing Coordination:**

- **Frequency:** 1 Marketing touchpoint every 30 days (e.g., LinkedIn / Google ads, direct mail).
- **Collaboration Trigger:** If Sales opts for a physical "Door Pull," Marketing increases touchpoints by +1, providing custom copy / inserts for the brochure leave-behind.

The SOP describes this cadence as *"a fixed rhythm of outreach designed to stay persistent without being intrusive."*

### Tracking & Reporting

From §7. The Master Tracking Spreadsheet is the source of truth:

- **Access permissions:**
  - **View Only** — general stakeholders (FYI purposes).
  - **Edit Access** — Marketing and Sales Leadership only.
- **Content:** Active and On-Deck accounts, ensuring the Rolling Three model is strictly followed.

The Master Spreadsheet link lives in the ABM SharePoint and is referenced (but not hardcoded) in the SOP.

## Named landmines

- **The "Generic Local" Claim.** Every regional ISP says "we're local." The differentiator is **named geography** — Blair, Nebraska NOC; 110+ years; 16,500-mile fiber footprint. Specificity sells.
- **The Stat Inflation.** A rep, trying to sound impressive, exaggerates a stat ("we serve thousands of businesses"). The third-party validations above are the ones that are sourced and defensible. Use those; don't invent.
- **The "All 10 Products" Recital.** The Business Capability deck lists 10 product lines because that's the portfolio map. Walking a customer through all 10 in a single call is feature-recital, not discovery. Pick 2–3 based on pre-call research; teaser one cross-sell; leave the rest for later.
- **The "We've Won J.D. Power" Claim Without Caveat.** The UC Competitor Battle Card references the J.D. Power Award for the **Intermedia platform** (the underlying UC technology) — not for GPC specifically. The honest framing: *"the underlying UC platform has won the J.D. Power Award nine years running for customer satisfaction."* Don't claim GPC has the award itself.
- **The "We Guarantee Zero Downtime" Trap.** Five nines uptime is 5.26 minutes of downtime per year — not zero. Customers (especially IT directors) catch reps who say "we never go down." Honest framing: *"99.999% uptime backed by SLA — that's less than six minutes a year, on average."*
